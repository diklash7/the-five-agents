# The Tiger Is Running Out of Planet

There are fewer than 6,000 tigers left on Earth. That's not a distant conservation projection — that's the 2022 IUCN count. And it gets sharper when you look at the trajectory: in the early 20th century, tigers roamed across much of Asia. Today, they've lost **at least 93% of their historic range**. Three subspecies are gone entirely. A fourth — the South China tiger — hasn't been seen in the wild since the 1970s.

This is what a slow-motion extinction looks like.

---

## What We're Actually Talking About

The tiger (*Panthera tigris*) is the largest wild cat on the planet. An apex predator built for the ambush — powerful shoulders, explosive acceleration, and a patience that most prey animals underestimate. Tigers are solitary, territorial animals. A single male can hold a range of up to 1,923 km². They swim rivers up to 8 km wide. Females raise two or three cubs and keep them close for roughly two years before they go it alone.

Nine subspecies were recognized historically. Today, only six survive in the wild:

- **Bengal** — India and surrounding areas, the most numerous
- **Siberian** — Russian Far East and northeastern China
- **Sumatran** — Indonesia, critically endangered
- **Indochinese** — mainland Southeast Asia
- **Malayan** — peninsular Malaysia
- **South China** — functionally extinct in the wild

The Caspian, Javan, and Bali tigers are gone. Not endangered. Gone.

## The Numbers, Country by Country

As of 2022, the global wild tiger population stands at an estimated **3,726–5,578 individuals**. That's down from 5,000–8,262 in the late 1990s. The habitat itself contracted: from 1,025,488 km² in 2001 to 911,901 km² in 2020 — a loss roughly the size of Ecuador in two decades.

India holds more than half the world's wild tigers. That's both a conservation success story and a reminder of how narrow the margin has become.

---

## Three Forces Driving the Decline

**1. Habitat destruction**
Deforestation, roads, railway lines, mining, irrigation channels — each project that cuts through a forest is another barrier a tiger can't cross. Fragmented habitat means isolated populations that can't interbreed, can't recover from losses, and slowly disappear.

**2. Poaching and illegal trade**
Tigers are killed for their skins and body parts, which move through illegal networks spanning at least 15 countries. Tiger bone products are traded for medicinal purposes across parts of Asia. Here's the wrinkle: "tiger farms" in China and Southeast Asia breed tigers commercially and market their parts as a legal alternative. Research consistently shows this makes things worse — it legitimizes demand rather than reducing it.

**3. Human–wildlife conflict**
Where natural prey has been depleted, tigers sometimes take livestock. Local communities kill them in retaliation. This isn't malice — it's economics. A farmer who loses a cow to a tiger and gets no compensation has no reason to protect the species. Conservation that ignores this reality doesn't last.

---

## What's Actually Working

The tiger has been **Endangered on the IUCN Red List since 1986**. It's listed under CITES Appendix I — meaning international trade in tigers and their parts is banned. Every range country has national-level protections on paper.

Some of them work in practice:

**India** launched Project Tiger in 1973. Today, 53 tiger reserves cover 75,796 km² across the country. India's tiger population has grown steadily over the past two decades.

**Nepal** introduced serious anti-poaching operations in 2010. The result: multiple consecutive years of zero poaching, and a national tiger population that **doubled in a decade**. That's a rare story of genuine recovery.

**Wildlife corridors** — strips of connected forest between protected areas — allow isolated populations to find each other. At least nine corridors have been established across Nepal and India's Terai Arc landscape. China and Russia signed a transboundary agreement in 2019 to protect shared tiger habitat along the Sino-Russian border.

---

## The Bottom Line

The tiger is not beyond saving. Nepal proved that. What it requires is consistency: sustained funding, genuine enforcement, and conservation strategies that work with local communities rather than against them.

What it cannot survive is complacency. Three subspecies went extinct in the 20th century not because saving them was impossible, but because it wasn't prioritized until too late.

The window is still open. But the range keeps shrinking.
