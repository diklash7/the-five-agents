# Tiger — Wikipedia

The tiger (*Panthera tigris*) is the largest wild cat species on Earth and one of the most recognizable animals on the planet. Native to Asia, it is known for its powerful muscular body, large head and paws, and distinctive orange coat with black vertical stripes. It is an apex predator and a keystone species in the ecosystems it inhabits.

## Biology and Behavior

Tigers are solitary animals that occupy large home ranges, which vary by sex and prey availability. Males defend territories ranging from 84–1,923 km², while female territories are smaller, typically 10–520 km². They are powerful swimmers, capable of crossing rivers up to 8 km wide. Tigers are primarily crepuscular and nocturnal hunters, ambushing prey — mainly ungulates — by stealth and explosive short-distance bursts of speed. Females typically give birth to two or three cubs, which remain with their mother for approximately two years before establishing independent territories.

## Subspecies

Historically, nine subspecies were recognized, including the Bengal, Siberian, Sumatran, Indochinese, Malayan, South China, Caspian, Javan, and Bali tigers. Three of these — the Caspian, Javan, and Bali tigers — are now extinct. The South China tiger has not been confirmed in the wild since the 1970s and survives only in captivity.

## Population and Range Decline

The numbers tell a stark story. The global wild tiger population is estimated at **3,726–5,578 individuals as of 2022**, down from an estimated 5,000–8,262 in the late 1990s. Since the early 20th century, tigers have lost **at least 93% of their historic range**. Their range has declined by 53–68% since the late 1990s alone. The tiger's habitat area shrank from 1,025,488 km² in 2001 to 911,901 km² in 2020.

By country (2022 estimates):
- **India**: 3,167–3,682 (the world's largest tiger population)
- **Russia**: 573–600
- **Indonesia**: 393
- **Nepal**: 316–355
- **All other range countries combined**: <500

## Threats

Three interlinked threats drive the decline:

1. **Habitat destruction and fragmentation** — deforestation, roads, railway lines, and mining have broken up forests that tigers need to survive and breed.
2. **Poaching** — tigers are killed for their skins and body parts, which fuel illegal trade networks spanning at least 15 countries. Tiger bone products are widely traded for medicinal purposes in parts of Asia.
3. **Human–wildlife conflict** — tigers occasionally prey on livestock in areas where natural prey has been depleted, leading to retaliatory killings by local communities.

"Tiger farm" facilities in China and Southeast Asia breed tigers commercially for their parts, but research suggests these operations worsen the problem by stimulating demand for tiger products rather than reducing pressure on wild populations.

## Conservation Efforts

The tiger has been listed as **Endangered on the IUCN Red List since 1986** and is protected under CITES Appendix I. National-level protections exist in every range country. India's Project Tiger, launched in 1973, has established 53 tiger reserves covering 75,796 km². Nepal's anti-poaching operations, launched in 2010, have led to multiple years of "zero poaching" and a doubling of the national tiger population over a decade. Wildlife corridors connecting protected areas have been established in Nepal, India, Sumatra, and along the China-Russia border — allowing fragmented populations to reconnect.

Despite these efforts, the species remains under severe pressure, and the recovery is fragile: three subspecies went extinct in the 20th century alone.
